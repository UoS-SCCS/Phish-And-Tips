Training Summary

Congratulations! You've completed the Phish and Tips training exercise. Below you will find further information on the different types of suspicious content you may have seen whilst completing the training. Click or touch the heading too see more information.

Once you've finished reading please click the Next button, where we will collect some usage information and provide an opportunity for you to restart the training if you wish to have another go.

The aim of the training was to encourage you to look at emails as a series of smaller parts and to evaluate each one separately. You may have seen examples of content we have labelled as suspicious in legitimate emails. This is to be expected to some degree.

Phishers and scammers look to replicate legitimate emails. A legitimate company may send you an email with an urgent message, whilst a phisher will use urgent language to try to drive you towards their scam website. This is one of the biggest challenges when evaluating phishing emails.

As such, the objective is not to classify the email as a whole, but to encourage better risk evaluation. If you see one or more suspicious parts to an email it does not necessarily mean the email is a scam or phishing email, but it should encourage greater caution and investigation on your part. If you are uncertain about whether an email is legitimate do not hesitate to check with the company directly or ask friends or relatives for their opinion.

When checking with a company make sure to use known good contact details, for example, visit the website using a previously saved bookmark or contact via a phone number from a previous bill, statement or correspondence. In particular, do not use the contact details, links or web addresses in the suspicious email itself as these may be part of the scam.

It is not uncommon to see spelling or grammar errors in phishing and scam emails. This is primarily due to the writers not being native English speakers. If you see spelling or grammar errors in an email it is a strong indicator that the email may not be legitimate, and requires additional caution.

The objective of most phishers and scammers is to get you to take an action you would not normally take, for example, visiting a different website or providing your log in details. They do this by creating a sense of urgency through techniques like threatening a service is about to terminate, you are about lose access to data or an account, or that an opportunity is about to be missed.

Such urgent language should raise suspicion but cannot be entirely excluded from legitimate emails. Unfortunately many legitimate companies are seeking to get you to take action as well, for example, visiting their site, buying a product from them, or signing up for new services. This can be one of the most difficult to distinguish, but in general, if you feel a sense of urgency take a moment to evaluate in more detail the email and if there are any doubts, contact the company directly to determine if the email is legitimate.

Do not be concerned about contacting a company directly to check. You are not wasting their time. If they are sending emails out that are difficult to distinguish from phishing and scam emails then that is their own fault and they should be doing better.

Phishing and scam emails will often look to disguise or hide the address of the website they are trying to get you to visit. There are a number of ways they can do this, ranging from registering similar looking or sounding addresses through to hiding the link behind a button or different text, for example, the text "Click Here".

You can see where a link is going to go to by hovering over it with your mouse and looking in the bottom left hand corner of the screen. It will show you where the link points to, which may be different to what is written in the text. Always check this value to make sure it is the address you expect and know for the company claiming to be the sender of the email address.

If it is different or looks unusual do not click the link. Visit the company directly using a known good link, for example, a bookmark or website address included in a previous bill or statement. If in doubt contact the company directly to check.

Phishers and scammers will exploit weaknesses in how email addresses are displayed to make an email look like it came from a different address - normally a reputable company or organisation - rather than the scammers real address. Many email providers are wise to this and mark such emails as spam, but it is worth being aware of in case a scam email gets through

To verify the From: address look at the headers of the email. In the case of the GMail this can be accessed by clicking the downwards arrow next to your name or address in the email header. This will show the additional headers, including the reply-to and possibly sender header. If these do not match the From: address it could be a scammer impersonating someone else.

Scammers and Phishers may not know your actual name, they may only have your email address. They still need to provide a greeting in the email so will often pick generic greatings, for example, Dear Valued Customer or To Our Newest VIP Member. These are designed to be flattering whilst still being entirely generic.
